# Chapter 1: A Photograph Missing the Photographer

Before the mountain trip, the last time all of us stood together was on the small stage at Bờ Kè.

Calling it a stage was generous. It was really just a wooden platform raised about two handspans above the floor, with just enough room for Kiệt’s drum set, Bảo’s electric piano, two old amplifiers, and a few tangled power cords at our feet. Every time someone stepped too hard, the platform creaked. Yellow light shone down from above, making everyone’s face look oily. The place smelled of roasted coffee, cheap beer, and hot wiring.

I played lead guitar. My job was to stand slightly off to the left, watch the fretboard, and make sure the solos were not obviously wrong. Kiệt sat behind the drums, thin, long-armed, steady when he played but always looking like he was counting debts. Bảo sat at the electric piano, back straight, fingers clean, eyes on the keys more than on people. And Đạt stood in the middle.

Đạt sang lead, played rhythm guitar, and made the rest of the show look like a show. He wore a light-brown acoustic guitar, the strap a little frayed, his shirt untucked, his hair deliberately messy. Before singing, he always had to say something to the audience, even when that audience had only a little over thirty people, half of them friends, the other half customers who had come in to escape the rain.

“The next song is for anyone who accidentally walked into this place because it was raining, and now has to listen to us sing until it stops”, Đạt said into the microphone.

There were a few laughs. Bảo glanced at him. Kiệt tapped his drumsticks lightly, making a badum-tss sound. I adjusted the volume knob on my guitar. Đạt turned toward me and raised an eyebrow.

“You ready, guitar hero?”

I said, “Don’t call me that.”

He laughed into the microphone. “Our lead guitarist is very modest. Actually, he’s just afraid of messing up.”

The audience laughed a little more. I looked down at the pedal by my foot. My jack cable was a bit loose, and every time it was touched it gave off a small crackle. Things like that always happened at Bờ Kè. Microphones squealed, drums drifted, the low B key on the piano sometimes did not respond, Đạt forgot lyrics but pretended he was handing the microphone to the audience to sing along even though no one knew the words. And yet the show kept going. Maybe because Đạt stood in the middle and made every small mistake seem intentional.

When the third song began, the place went quieter. It was a song Đạt had written, or at least he said he had. The chords were simple, Bảo added a few thin piano notes at the beginning, Kiệt came in very softly on the drums, and I waited until the chorus to play lead. Đạt leaned close to the microphone. His voice was not truly beautiful, a little hoarse, short of breath in places, but easy to listen to because he believed in every line he sang. He had a way of making people think ordinary sentences were worth keeping.

I watched him from the left side of the stage. Đạt closed his eyes on the last line of the first verse, his right hand strumming evenly, his wrist loose. Yellow light clung to the bridge of his nose. Below, a girl held up her phone to record. Two people at the table near the door stopped talking. Bảo kept his eyes on the keys, but the corner of his mouth lifted slightly. Kiệt played the hi-hat with the tip of his stick, very softly.

Đạt opened his eyes just as I came in for the solo. He stepped back half a pace, giving space to the sound of my guitar, then turned toward the audience, his hand still keeping rhythm. I hated moments like that because they made everything seem sequential. As if all each person had to do was play their part correctly and the song would go where it needed to go. Reality was not like that. A string could snap, a speaker could cut out, customers could leave, one wrong note could pull three more wrong notes after it. But for a little over four minutes, beneath the hot yellow light and the weak air conditioning, Đạt made people believe everything was fitting together.

At the end of the song, the café applauded. Not loudly, but fully enough. Đạt bowed too deeply, like an artist in the city theater. Kiệt threw a drumstick up and almost failed to catch it. Bảo cut the piano sound before the note had finished ringing, frowning because he thought Đạt was overacting. I pulled the pick out from between my fingers and put it in my pocket.

After the show, we sat at a table on the veranda. The rain had just stopped, and water dripped from the corrugated roof into a plastic bucket beside the door, each drop landing with a hollow plop. Đạt was still excited. He opened his phone to watch the clips people had recorded, scrubbing back and forth to the exact part where the audience clapped.

“This part is good”, he said. “Looks like we’re about to get famous.”

Bảo drank water and said, “Famous within a three-coffee-table radius.”

“Three tables today, who knows, next month it could be four.”

Kiệt laughed. “Tomorrow the place will probably close for electrical repairs.”

Đạt pointed at him. “Every sentence you say brings bad luck. That’s why you’re assigned to sit all the way in the back.”

“Where else would the drums sit if not in the back?”

“In the audience’s lap.”

Bảo almost choked on his water. I sat wiping my guitar strings with an old cloth. The fingers on my left hand still hurt from pressing too much. The tip of my index finger had a hard callus, darker than the skin around it. Đạt saw it and took a picture right away.

“An artist’s hand”, he said.

“More like the hand of someone who forgot to cut his nails”, I replied.

“That’s also a kind of artist.”

Đạt posted that photo to his story, using the song we had just played as the background music. I saw it appear on my own screen for a few seconds, then swiped past. At the time, I did not think much of it. What was there to keep about a hand with uncut nails and a callus on the fingertip? Đạt seemed different. He kept everything in photographs, as if all he had to do was press the shutter and a moment would agree to stay still there.

That night, as the group was getting ready to pack up, Đạt suddenly suggested we take a trip somewhere that weekend. He said lately we had been circling endlessly around classrooms, rented rooms, cafés, and crackly recording files until he was sick of it. We needed to get out for a day, climb somewhere a little higher than the city, take a few decent photos.

“Want to climb a mountain?” he asked.

Bảo looked at him. “You just finished performing, so now you think you’re the main character?”

Đạt slapped his chest. “I’ve always been the main character. You guys are just slow to realize it.”

Kiệt took out his phone to look up directions. “Where?”

Đạt sent a link into the group chat right there at the table. The pictures in the post were beautiful. People stood on a rocky outcrop with a valley and white clouds behind them. Everyone was smiling as if they had just escaped some small, cramped life.

I looked at the screen. My guitar was propped beside the chair, its third string still slightly out of tune. Outside the alley, rainwater ran in a thin stream down into the drain. A skinny cat walked past, stopped to sniff the bucket catching rainwater, then left.

“Coming?” Đạt asked me.

I asked, “What time do we get back?”

“Afternoon. Easy trail. Lots of people go. It’ll be good for photos.”

Bảo said, “Can I bring the piano?”

“Sure. You carry it up, I’ll sing.”

Kiệt tapped two fingers on the tabletop in a meaningless rhythm. I looked at Đạt. He still had the guitar strap over his shoulder, his hair damp with sweat, his eyes bright from an idea that had just sparked and had not yet been cooled by anyone.

I had no reason to refuse. That weekend I had only planned to do laundry, sleep in, and change my guitar strings if I remembered.

“I’ll go”, I said.

Đạt raised a hand like he had just closed a major deal.

“That’s the spirit of a band”, he said. “Not just playing music. We need material to remember later.”

“Memories of three coffee tables”, Bảo said.

Kiệt added, “And a piano on a mountain.”

Đạt laughed. His laughter carried out onto the veranda, mixing with the sound of water dripping from the corrugated roof into the plastic bucket. Back then, everything was still in its proper place. Đạt stood in the middle, Bảo nitpicked, Kiệt kept rhythm with his fingers, and I sat on the left with my guitar. The rain outside the alley had stopped completely. Inside the café, yellow light still fell over the small wooden platform, making the cords at our feet look like tangled lines. Đạt bent down to pick up his guitar bag, his mouth still smiling because of the joke about the piano on the mountain.

At that moment, he was only laughing at a bad joke.

And we let him laugh.

On the morning of the trip, he arrived fifteen minutes late. Đạt wore an orange jacket and sunglasses even though the sky was still overcast. As soon as he got out of the ride-hailing car, he lifted both hands like a celebrity stepping onto the red carpet. Bảo cursed him for showing off. Kiệt laughed. I stood beside a planter, holding a bag of bánh mì, watching a small caterpillar crawl along the edge of a leaf.

Đạt came over and broke off half of my baguette.

“You look like you were forced to be here.”

I said, “I’m just hungry.”

He took a big bite and chewed very naturally. “Now you’re hungry and missing bread. More layered emotionally.”

On the coach, Đạt sat by the window and photographed things as they passed. Utility poles. A phở shop. A dog lying in front of a motorbike repair shop. A sign saying “authentic bún bò” with the tones written wrong. After every shot he would look, delete it, then take another. Bảo dozed off, his head hitting the window twice. Kiệt wore earphones but played the sound out loud. I ate the rest of the bread and put the limp cucumber slices into the paper bag.

The trail up the mountain was crowded at first. There was a family with a child who kept demanding to be carried. A group of students in matching shirts sang off beat as they walked. A few older people used walking sticks, stepping slowly but steadily. The path was red earth mixed with stone, with tree roots rising on both sides like veins on the back of a hand. After the rain the night before, the air was damp and heavy with the smell of rotting leaves.

Đạt walked ahead, then turned back to photograph us. He told Bảo to look less serious, told Kiệt to lift his face, told me to “stop looking at nature like it owes you money.”

Later, that photograph would be reposted many times. In it, Bảo is giving a thumbs-up, Kiệt is grinning with his teeth showing, and I am standing off at the far left, squinting because of the glare. Đạt is not in the frame because he was the one holding the camera. At the time, no one noticed that. A photograph missing the photographer was still an ordinary thing.

The higher we climbed, the wetter the path became. In some places, stepping down made a sticky squelch. Kiệt reminded everyone to walk closer to the inside. Bảo said he knew, but still stepped toward the edge to avoid a puddle. Đạt wore dark gray hiking shoes with a green stripe on the side. He bragged that he had bought them on sale, that the soles gripped well, and that after the trip he would give them a five-star review if he was still alive to get home.

Bảo snapped, “Talk properly.”

Đạt laughed. “All I have to do is say one sentence and everyone has something to keep talking about.”

At a wide patch of ground overlooking the mountainside, we stopped to rest. The wind blew harder, carrying the smell of fresh mud and crushed grass. Another group had just left, leaving shoeprints pressed into the soft ground and a blue bottle cap. Đạt picked up the cap and put it in his pocket, muttering, “Let’s be more civilized, humanity.”

Then he took out his phone and said we needed one photo of the whole group walking, natural style, not looking at the camera. Kiệt said that sounded cheesy. Đạt replied that cheesiness was what helped humans endure life. Kiệt handed me a water bottle. The water was already warm and tasted like plastic.

Đạt stepped backward to find the angle. Looking at the screen, he said, “Stay right there. Bảo, put your hand down. Kiệt, your face looks like you owe somebody money.”

I saw the heel of his shoe land on the edge of the path. That edge was covered in grass. Under it was a layer of dark soil from the rain. I was about to say be careful. The first word had not left my mouth when Đạt’s left foot slipped down.

There was no pause. Not one second stretched long enough for anyone to grab anyone’s hand. His body tilted, his hand flailed upward, his phone flew into the rocks. The orange jacket vanished from sight. Then came the heavy sound of falling, the crackling snap of branches breaking, then silence.

Bảo screamed. Kiệt stood frozen in place.

I lay down at the edge of the path and looked down. Đạt was caught in a low bush, not very far, but the slope was slippery and full of rocks. I climbed down first because I was standing near a place with roots to hold onto. I do not think I was brave. It was just that if we kept standing up there, Đạt would still be lying down there.

Earth stuck to my palms. My knee hit a rock. When I reached him, I called his name. Đạt’s eyes were open but not looking directly at me. There was blood at his mouth. His breaths were short, torn, sounding like someone squeezing a punctured plastic bag.

I said, “Stay still.”

He did not answer. His lips moved. I bent closer, but I could only hear the wind, Bảo calling from above, Kiệt’s voice repeating rescue instructions into the phone. I did not know what Đạt wanted to say. So later, when someone asked what his last words were, I said I had not heard clearly.

I pressed a towel against the bleeding wound on his forehead. Blood soaked through the cloth very quickly. Đạt’s right arm lay at the wrong angle. An ant crawled over his wrist, reached the wet streak, then turned around. I brushed it away.

Đạt kept breathing for a while. Bảo said almost twenty minutes. Kiệt said probably only ten. I am not sure who was right. I remember my hands going numb. I remember a drop of water falling from a leaf onto Đạt’s cheek. I remember his chest moving, then stopping. I called his name a few more times. The last time, I called because there were still people up there waiting for me to say something.

Rescue arrived late. They had reasons. The road was far, the signal weak, that section of slope difficult to climb down. All of it was reasonable. Đạt still died.

# Chapter 2: Don’t Make It More Beautiful

The funeral was crowded. He knew more people than I had thought. Or maybe death makes people remember that they once knew someone. Đạt’s mother stood beside the coffin, eyes swollen, hair hastily clipped behind her neck. She held my hand longer than she held anyone else’s.

“You were next to him then?”

“Yes.”

“Was he alone?”

“No, cô.”

She nodded. Her hand was dry and cold. She cried but did not ask anything more. I was grateful I did not have to say things I did not know.

After the funeral, Đạt appeared everywhere. On personal pages, in stories, in the temporary profile pictures of people who had once gone out to eat with him. Everyone had their own sentence. “You lived so brightly.” “You left while doing what you loved.” “Your youth will always be beautiful.” “The final trip.” Someone wrote that Đạt had finished his song, still bright, still on beat, still the center the way he had been onstage.

I read those lines in my rented room. The fan ran on speed two, clicking because the cage was crooked. On the table sat a cup of instant noodles that had swollen for too long. The sentences written about Đạt were soft, bright, clean. In them, he was no longer the guy who had once forgotten lyrics and blamed the audience, who had teased Kiệt, who had borrowed money from me and taken two weeks to return it, who had taken twelve photos of one pair of shoes just to choose one to post. He had been wiped free of mud, color-corrected, placed somewhere beautiful.

I understood why people did it. The living need a way to get through the day. They need a sentence light enough to carry into class, meals, sleep. I just could not carry that sentence.

A week later, Bảo messaged the group, saying we should keep that trip as a beautiful memory. Đạt had been very happy. Đạt had always wanted people to live fully. If he saw the whole group falling apart, he would probably be sad.

Kiệt reacted with a heart. Then he wrote, “Yeah, I think Đạt would want that.”

I stared at that sentence for a very long time. In my head was Đạt down the slope, mouth full of blood, eyes open, breath breaking apart. I saw nothing there that looked like a choice.

I typed, “Đạt didn’t want to die there. He just slipped.”

Bảo replied with a very long message. He said I was cold, cruel, and convinced I was the only clear-headed one. He said everyone was hurting, not just me. He asked why I could not let the dead rest.

I wrote, “I’m only saying it was an accident.”

Bảo called. I let it ring until it stopped. Kiệt messaged me privately: “Don’t say that right now. Bảo is really angry.”

I replied, “I know.”

Kiệt wrote, “If you know, then stop.”

From then on, people began avoiding me. Bảo did it most obviously. When we crossed paths at school, he looked elsewhere. During group assignments, he sent his part but not one extra word. Kiệt was slower, then he drifted away too. Whenever someone mentioned Đạt, they glanced at me first, as if I were a glass of water placed too close to the edge of a table. Able to fall at any moment.

I learned to stand up and go buy water whenever Đạt’s name appeared.

Once, in the cafeteria, a classmate told me that everyone had their own way of grieving, but mine hurt other people. She said it very gently, eyes lowered to her peach tea. I asked what she had heard me say. She said she did not remember exactly, but roughly that I had denied the meaning of Đạt’s death. I kept eating my rice. The fried egg that day was salty and had too many scallions. She waited for me to answer, so I said, “I don’t know whether death needs meaning in order to be grieved.”

She fell silent, then said, “The way you talk is scary.”

I nodded. Not because I agreed. I just wanted the meal to end.

Nearly a month later, the group chat lit up in the afternoon. It was Đạt’s birthday. Bảo invited everyone to Bờ Kè. The message ended with the sentence: “Anyone who still considers Đạt a friend should come.”

I was waiting for the washing machine on the ground floor of my building. Clothes beat against the drum with a heavy thud-thud. I looked at that line, then typed, “I’ll come.”

The café still had yellow lights, the creaking wooden platform, the cords tangled together at the feet of the amplifiers. Kiệt’s drum set was no longer there. Neither was Bảo’s electric piano. On the small stage, the owner had placed a vase of fake flowers and a portable speaker. It looked wider than before, as if all it took was for a few objects to be missing for a familiar place to reveal its emptiness very quickly.

When I arrived, everyone was already seated. Bảo sat at the innermost seat, an unstirred glass of bạc xỉu in front of him. Kiệt was by the window. A few classmates and a few people who had known Đạt nodded to greet me. No one said my name.

At first they told old stories. Đạt being late. Đạt forgetting lyrics. Đạt calling me guitar hero. Đạt pretending to bow like a great artist even when that day’s audience was almost gone. Someone laughed, then wiped their eyes. I sat and listened. A mosquito landed on my hand. I blew it away. It came back to the rim of my glass.

Bảo opened a clip. Sad music, white text on a black background. “There are people who never disappear.” “A complete youth.” “Thank you for choosing to burn bright.”

In the clip, Đạt smiled onstage, sang, strummed, bowed to the audience. Then came scenes of him running on the mountain path, tying his shoelaces, flashing a V sign. Then the group photo appeared, the one he was not in.

Bảo turned off his phone and looked at me.

“What do you think?”

I knew what shape the answer was supposed to have. Good. Moving. Đạt would be happy if he saw it. Those sentences were already lying on the table like napkins folded into triangles.

“It’s fine”, I said.

Bảo gave a thin smile. “Just fine?”

“Yeah.”

A girl said softly, “Come on.”

Bảo placed his phone face down. “I’m asking honestly. Even now, you still think we’re wrong, don’t you?”

“I didn’t say wrong.”

“Then what is it?”

“I just think Đạt didn’t choose that.”

Bảo looked at me for a moment, then laughed. The laugh was very short, not like a laugh. Kiệt lowered his face. Outside the alley, a motorbike passed, its exhaust echoing against the wall before fading out.

“You’re really something”, he said. “You always talk like you’re writing an incident report. Soft ground, slipped, rescue arrived late, then dead. End of story. Sounds so neat, doesn’t it?”

I felt my neck grow hot.

“I’m not saying it to make it neat.”

“Then what are you saying it for?”

I did not answer right away.

Bảo leaned forward, both hands braced against the edge of the table. “Do you know how you make us feel? Like we’re idiots. Like we made up pretty little lines to fool ourselves.”

“I never said you were idiots.”

“But you make us feel that way.”

One of Đạt’s female friends added, “Everyone is hurting. Would it kill you to say it a little more gently?”

Someone else said, “Being right is not always the same as being kind.”

Bảo nodded, eyes still on me. “What kind of life are you living? Is Đạt, to you, nothing but soft ground, a slipped foot, rescue arriving late? Is there nothing else left to remember?”

The ice in a glass knocked together with a small clink. I heard that sound more clearly than their voices.

“Then how do you want me to remember?” I asked.

No one answered immediately. The question stalled the whole table, not because it was difficult, but because if they said it out loud, it would sound like a demand. Kiệt looked away. Someone sitting near the edge of the table lowered their head and stirred a glass even though the ice had almost melted.

Bảo looked at me for a long time. His eyes were red, but his voice dropped lower.

“Remember him as a friend”, he said. “Not as an accident.”

“I haven’t forgotten he was a friend.”

“You have”, Bảo said. “Every time you talk, Đạt disappears. Only the slope is left, the soft ground, the late rescue. Only those things.”

I wanted to say that for me, those things did not replace Đạt. They were the last things still stuck to him. Orange jacket, blood, mud, short breaths. But I knew that if I said it, everyone at the table would hear it as me trying to hurt them more.

“Then what has to be added?” I asked. “Music? A caption? A sentence that feels better?”

Bảo’s face changed. Not much, just as if someone had pulled hard on a string inside him.

“That’s you. Your friend died in front of you and you insist on saying it was meaningless. You have to drag everything down to the ground before you’re satisfied.”

I was about to say I was not dragging anything down. About to say it was already on the ground. About to stand up and leave. But those sentences got stuck, breaking into something else.

“He really was on the ground.”

The whole table went silent.

I looked at Bảo, then at the faces around me. Everyone was waiting for me to stop. But I had stopped too many times already.

“He was lying down there”, I said. “His jacket was muddy, his mouth was full of blood. He wasn’t bright. He didn’t choose anything. He wasn’t smiling. He was trying to breathe. Do you understand? He was trying to breathe.”

Bảo shot to his feet. The chair hit the wall.

“Shut up.”

I did not shut up.

“You keep forcing him to keep performing. Forcing him to smile in clips, forcing him to become youth, a lesson, a beautiful memory. You wrap his death up so it’s easier to hold, then tell me I have to hold it that way too. I can’t. I was down there. I heard him breathing. I know he didn’t want to die.”

Someone called my name. Someone’s voice trembled. I still heard myself continue.

“I’m not making Đạt uglier. I just don’t want him turned into a caption.”

Bảo came around the table and grabbed my collar. His face was red, his eyes wet.

“You think you’re the only one hurting?”

“I never said I hurt more.”

“Then what do you want?”

That question fell between us. What do you want. What is being right for? What is truth used for? The living always need an answer to know where the next step should go.

I looked at Bảo’s hand gripping my collar.

“I want you to stop saying he chose to die.”

Bảo let go as if he had touched something dirty.

“Get out.”

I picked up my backpack. As I turned to leave, my hip bumped the table, spilling iced tea across it. Someone quickly lifted their phone to keep it from getting wet. The movement was fast and precise, as if that night the only thing left worth saving was the phone.

I left the café. The alley was hotter than inside. A cat lay under a motorbike, opened its eyes to look at me, then closed them again. The messages arrived while I was waiting at a red light.

Bảo: “From now on, don’t show your face in front of me again.”

Someone else: “You really went too far.”

Kiệt messaged last: “I understand part of it, but tonight you made everything worse.”

The light turned green. Traffic surged forward. I stood there a few more seconds, then crossed the road.

That night, back in my room, I saw that my collar had been stretched. There was a red mark on my collarbone. I washed my face. At first the water was hot, then it slowly turned cold. In the mirror, my face did not look like the face of someone who had won or lost. It was just a face that needed sleep.

I opened the group chat. Bảo had left. A few others followed. Kiệt stayed for about ten more minutes, then disappeared from the member list. In the end, only I and Đạt’s account remained.

His profile picture was still the one taken at the hotpot place, eyes narrowed because of the steam, mouth wide in a smile. The message input box was empty. I placed my finger on the keyboard, but there was nothing to send. The dead did not need me to defend them. The living did not need me to tell the truth that way either.

# Chapter 3: Soft Ground 

Two days later, Đạt’s mother called. Her voice was small, as if coming from a room full of furniture.

“Are you free? I want to ask you a few things about that day.”

I went to Đạt’s house in the late afternoon. There were several snake plants by the front door. On the living room table sat his photograph, a small bowl of incense, and a plate of oranges. Đạt’s mother made tea but did not drink it. She took out a sheet of paper folded into quarters, its edges softened from being opened too many times.

“The tourist site said you kids left the safe path”, she said. “They said there was a warning sign there. I don’t remember seeing any sign in the photos. I don’t want to argue to get anything out of them. I just want to know what’s true.”

I looked at the sheet of paper. It had a few printed lines, a red stamp, sentences that sounded too clean. Visitor carelessness. Area with warning. Unforeseen accident.

“There was no sign at that section”, I said.

She looked at me.

“Did you climb outside the path?”

“No. We were standing on the walkway. Đạt stepped back to take a photo. The edge was covered in grass. The earth was soft.”

She nodded very slowly. She did not cry. The fact that she did not cry made the room heavier. A wall clock ticked above the cabinet. The sound was so even it became irritating.

“Can you tell me again?” she asked. “Piece by piece. I know it will be hard to hear.”

I told her. I did not add music. I did not add last words. I did not remove the blood. I said Đạt kept breathing for a while, said I pressed a towel to his forehead, said he was not alone. I said rescue arrived late. I said the slope was slippery. I said the grassy edge had looked like firm ground but sank underneath.

Đạt’s mother sat listening, both hands placed on her knees. The nail of her thumb was split. When I reached the part where Đạt’s chest stopped moving, she bowed her head. Her shoulders shook once, then stopped.

I stopped speaking.

She asked, “Was he scared?”

I looked at the plate of oranges on the table. One of them was bruised near the stem.

“Maybe”, I said. “I’m not sure. But I called his name. I was there.”

She closed her eyes. Tears ran down, but her voice stayed clear.

“Thank you for not making him more beautiful.”

I did not know how to answer.

She took another envelope from the drawer. Inside were several photos friends had sent. All of them were bright, color-corrected, chosen at the exact moment Đạt smiled. Among the stack was one slightly crooked photo. Đạt was sitting on a rock, bent over to tie his gray shoes with the green stripe. His hair was messy from the wind. His mouth was slightly open, probably in the middle of saying something. Behind him was the damp dirt path, the grass edge darkened.

“I like this one”, she said. “It looks like him at home. He always tied his shoes twice.”

She handed me the photo. “Keep one. You don’t need to post it. Just keep it.”

I took it. For the first time since Đạt died, there was a photo of him that did not make me angry. It did not force him to be radiant. It let him bend down and tie his shoes, ordinary, a little messy, alive without yet knowing he was about to become a memory.

Before I left, Đạt’s mother said, “I don’t need people to tell me he left beautifully. I need people to know that place is dangerous.”

She stopped for a moment.

“What is beauty for, if next year another kid steps back too?”

That sentence followed me all the way down the alley. It was not like the sentences online. It was not soft. It had edges.

A month later, I wrote the report. I wrote it very slowly. Soft ground. No railing. No visible warning sign at the resting section. Rescue arrived after the victim had already stopped breathing. I reread it many times, changing any words that could make the matter sound more serious or less serious than it was. I did not want to win. I did not want anyone to lose either. I only wanted the words to stand in the right place.

How Bảo found out about it, I do not know. He messaged me a single line: “Are you satisfied now?”

I looked at the message, then turned off my phone. Not every question needs an answer. Some people need to keep Đạt in a clip with music. Some need to keep him in an unedited photograph. Some need a signed sheet of paper. None of it can bring him back to life. But some ways can make him die one more time.

A few months later, my shoes finally broke for good. The sole on the right had come loose at the toe, making a small flapping sound with each step. After class, I stopped by a store near the bus station. The air conditioning inside was too strong, and the smell of new rubber mixed with cardboard dried my throat.

The salesperson asked whether I needed running shoes or hiking shoes.

“Shoes for a lot of walking”, I said. “Something durable.”

He led me to the shelf inside. On the second row was a dark gray pair, mid-cut, with a green stripe on the side.

I stood looking at it.

In my head appeared Đạt bending down to tie his shoes on the rock. Then Đạt stepping back. His heel landing on the grassy edge. No music. No last words. Just one small movement, then he vanished from sight.

The salesperson brought out my size. I tried them on. The soles gripped well. The toe was a little stiff, but it would probably soften after a few days.

“Will you take this pair?”

I bent down to tie the laces. The knot sat neatly on the top of my foot.

“I’ll take them.”

I did not buy them to remember Đạt. Nor to atone for anything. My old pair was broken. This pair fit. The price was thirty percent off.

The following week, I returned to the mountain alone.

I did not tell anyone. The morning coach had few passengers. The seat beside me was empty. Outside the window, food shops were opening, steam rising from pots of broth, a man watering plants in front of his house. I wore the new shoes. In my backpack were a bottle of water, a thin raincoat, and the photo Đạt’s mother had given me. I did not know why I brought it. Maybe because it had already been lying in my notebook. Maybe because I did not want to leave it in the room.

The trail up the mountain was still crowded. There were still people wearing white shoes, still groups of students talking and laughing loudly, still a child demanding to be carried. A woman selling water at the foot of the slope asked whether I was going alone. I nodded. She handed me a bottle of water, gave me my change, then turned to call another customer. No one recognized me. The mountain did not either.

I walked slowly. The new shoes were stiff around my ankles. Some sections of earth had dried and cracked into thin lines. In some places, they had poured in more gravel. I recognized the bend with the roots rising from the ground, recognized the open patch where the blue bottle cap had been, recognized the smell of rotting leaves when the wind blew back up from below.

When I reached the place where Đạt fell, I stopped.

The edge of the path had been staked with wooden posts and strung with rope. It was not beautiful. The rope sagged a little in the middle, and the right-hand post leaned slightly. Beside it was a new sign, white background, red letters:

SOFT GROUND 
DO NOT STAND CLOSE TO THE EDGE
DO NOT STEP BACK WHILE TAKING PHOTOS

I looked at the sign for a very long time.

There was no Đạt’s name. No date of death. No sentence about youth. People passing by only glanced at it before continuing up. A group of friends stopped nearby to take photos. The one holding the camera stepped back, and the person beside him tugged his shirt and pointed at the sign.

“Hey, careful.”

The other one laughed and stepped forward.

That was all.

I stood behind them, hands in my jacket pockets. The wind blew past, carrying the smell of dry earth and human sweat. Down the slope, the bush that had held Đạt had been trimmed back. There was no orange jacket anymore. No blood. No ant on his wrist. Only a slope of earth, a few stones, and grass growing again in places that had seemed trampled beyond repair.

I took the photo from my backpack. In it, Đạt was still bending down to tie his shoes. His mouth slightly open. Hair messy. He knew nothing about the sign, about the report, about Bảo, about his mother, about me standing here in shoes that were like his by accident and not entirely by accident.

I did not leave the photo there. Did not place flowers. Did not take a picture to send anyone. I only looked a while longer, then put the photo back into my backpack.

Before leaving, I bent down to retie my left shoelace. The earth beneath my feet was harder than I remembered. Maybe because the rainy season had passed. Maybe because they had packed in more stones. Maybe because my memory made everything softer.

When I stood up, I looked at the sign once more. It was ugly. The printed letters were crooked. The posts had not been hammered straight. But if one day it made someone stop before taking one more step back, then Đạt did not need to be any brighter than that.

I turned down the mountain.

Behind me, someone laughed loudly. Someone else told them to stand still for a photo. The wind blew through the rope, making it tremble lightly. I walked slowly, eyes on the path. The new shoes gripped the earth very firmly.
