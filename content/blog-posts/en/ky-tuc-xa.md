# Chapter 1: Dormitory

As usual, I came back after a whole tiring day of studying. I, fortunately enough, got into a university considered one of the top Engineering schools in the South Vietnam, and I was even studying Electrical Engineering. I often used that as something to hold onto to get through the day, especially during the times when I felt like there was nothing special about me besides the school name stuck on my student card. Every time I walked through the dormitory hallway, looking at the half-open doors, hearing people laughing, the sound of games, the sound of instant noodles boiling, I reminded myself that at least I was here, in a place many people wanted to get into, studying a major whose name alone already sounded difficult, and if it was tiring, painful, or far from home, then at least it was a kind of suffering that had value.

When I got to the front of my room, I deliberately looked inside the shared bathroom beside it to see if he was there. The door was closed again, and there was a shadow of someone inside. I thought to myself, “Yeah, he is doing that depraved thing again.”

“He” here is my roommate. He studied Economics, what exact field I did not know, and I did not bother asking that bastard. He was an asshole in a very quiet way. Not loud, not aggressive, not the type to fight, but always making other people uncomfortable. He often dodged the room’s electricity and water bills. Ask him to go buy shared drinking water, he would not. Every time he was reminded, he would give a faint smile and say things like “current liquidity is a bit low” or “I will transfer it later.” I used to think, I do not know if this was just his own personality or some tactic of those Economics guys, turning the act of owing other people money into an operating model.

I did not like him. More accurately, I hated him in the way that the longer I lived with him, the more I hated him. His appearance was very normal, average height, a bit muscular, short hair brushed up, always wearing dark T-shirts and old sports pants. But there was something different about him. Not different like an eccentric genius, and not different like someone obviously dangerous. He was different in the kind of way where if you looked for one second, you saw nothing, but if you looked for ten seconds, you started feeling uncomfortable, and if you looked any longer, you suddenly did not want to stay in the same room with him at night.

I usually slept late. At night I often drew or played fighting games. I liked characters with long combos, liked the feeling of practicing the same sequence of movements over and over until my fingers remembered before my head did. There were nights when I sat until two or three in the morning just to practice one punish that I probably would not even be able to use in a real match. During those times, he would usually lie behind my bed, or sit at his desk, open a thick notebook and write something in very tiny handwriting. I did not know what he wrote. But I knew that sometimes he looked at me, not at the screen, but at my hands pressing buttons.

One night, after I lost repeatedly and lightly slammed down on the table, he suddenly said, “You react like a moron.”

I turned to him. “What?”

He did not look at me. He still lowered his head and wrote in the notebook. “Humans think they are choosing, but most of the time it is just a reaction repeated enough times.”

I gave a mocking laugh. “Are you studying Economics or fortune-telling?”

He raised his head and looked at me. His eyes under the desk lamp looked gray and flat. “Are those two different?”

From that day on, I found him even more sick. Not sick in the way that needed a hospital, but sick in the way he looked at humans like objects. He did not pay the electricity and water bills on time, but not because he was poor. Once I saw quite a lot of cash in his wallet. He deliberately did not pay. He waited until other people looked, waited to see who would look first, how many times they looked, whether they looked annoyed, whether they felt awkward, whether they let it go. After each time like that, he would write in the notebook again.

One time I lost my temper and snatched his notebook. He did not react strongly. He just sat still and looked at me as if that too had already been predicted. In the notebook, there was a very neatly hand-drawn table. The first column was names. The next column was the amount of money. The column after that was the amount of time endured before asking for it back. Then “level of discomfort,” “level of conflict avoidance,” “ability to ignore small losses.” My name was near the bottom of the page. Next to my name was a line that made my spine go cold: “Prideful, easily stimulated when told he chose the wrong major.”

I threw the notebook onto the table. “Are you insane?”

He picked the notebook up, smoothed out the folded page, then said very softly, “No. I am just learning.”

# Chapter 2: Bathroom

The thing that I truly found the strangest was still the bathroom. It was right beside my room door, close enough that every time someone opened the door, dragged their slippers, flushed water or stood in front of the sink, I could hear it clearly as if the sound was happening inside my own room. He went into the bathroom a lot at night. Sometimes at twelve, sometimes at one thirty, sometimes nearly three in the morning. Every time, he would step down from the bed very lightly, walk past me, open the room door, then almost immediately disappear behind the bathroom door beside it. After that came the sound of the door locking. Not loud, but very clear. A dry and neat “click,” unpleasant enough that I only needed to hear it once to know right away that it was him.

What annoyed me was not the fact that he went to the bathroom often. It was a dormitory, everyone had their own habits. What made me uncomfortable was that he stayed in there for too long. Sometimes fifteen minutes. Sometimes half an hour. Sometimes long enough that I thought he had fallen asleep in there. But inside there was no sound at all. No sound of water running. No sound of flushing. No sound of brushing teeth. No coughing. Only the silence stretching behind the thin door, right next to my room, as if he was not really doing anything in there besides standing still.

A few times, I pressed my ear against the wall, not exactly because I was curious, but because that uncomfortable feeling kept growing each night. I heard something very small, very slow, a sound like the tip of a fingernail tapping on glass. Knock. Knock. Knock. Then silence. Then knock again. I thought maybe he was standing in front of the mirror. Maybe he was looking at his face. Maybe he was squeezing pimples. Maybe he was doing some sick thing I did not want to imagine. But the more I thought that, the more wrong it felt, because if he was only looking in the mirror, why did he need to lock the door for so long, and why did his face look so disgustingly calm every time he stepped out, as if he had just finished negotiating with something inside.

I once asked him about that. He only said, “There are things that have to be locked from the outside.”

“What things?”

He looked toward the bathroom. “The thing that imitates.”

I did not ask anymore. Not because I believed it. But because I hated the way he said that sentence like an idiot.

# Chapter 3: Metamorphosis

After that was the push-up thing. The section of hallway near the bathroom had a broken light. At night that place was so dark that people could only see shapes, not faces. And yet he often went out there to train. He did push-ups very slowly, evenly to an unpleasant degree, not breathing heavily, not resting, not wiping sweat. I often went outside to buy water from the vending machine, and accidentally saw him a few times. He lowered himself, pushed up, stood, turned its neck, dropped its shoulders, then lowered itself again. The movement did not look like physical training. It looked like someone learning how to operate a body, learning from the beginning how a human should get tired, should breathe, should stand, should have trembling hands after exertion.

One night, I drew him. Not on purpose. I was sketching a character from Danganronpa, but my hand naturally remembered the shape of his back in the hallway, thin, bent, pitch black, both hands pressed against the tile floor. When I shaded the shadow, I realized there was something standing right behind him in the drawing. That thing had no face, only a smooth white space like paper that had not been drawn on. But that hand was placed on his shoulder very gently, almost intimately. I was certain I did not draw that hand.

I closed the app. I could not sleep all night.

Around nearly three in the morning, he got up again. I pretended to look at the laptop screen but was actually watching him through the reflection. He went out the door again. I waited a few seconds, then followed. When I cracked the room door open and looked out, he was standing in front of the bathroom door, one hand on the handle, head slightly lowered. Then the sound of the lock rang out. Click. Very small. Very neat. He did not step in right away. He stood still like that for a few seconds, face close to the door, as if listening to something whispering from inside.

Then, he turned his head back.

In the darkness, I could not see his face clearly, but I knew he was looking straight at me. Not the way someone startled after being caught would look. Not the way someone annoyed at being watched would look. He looked at me very calmly, as if from the beginning he had known I would be standing there, at exactly that time, in exactly that position. Then he smiled.

“Want to know what is in there?”

I did not answer.

He opened the door. Inside the bathroom was bright white. The long mirror reflected the row of empty sinks. He stepped in again, but in the mirror, his image did not step along. That image still stood outside the door, looking at me. I froze. The real him had vanished from the doorway, but his reflection in the mirror remained, slightly tilting its head, as if evaluating me.

Then from behind me, I heard his voice say, “Do not look too long. It learns quickly.”

I spun around. He was standing right behind me, his hand still in that posture. I looked into the bathroom. Empty. I looked into the mirror. In there was me, him, and another me standing deeper behind, wearing my shirt, holding my drawing notebook, but the face had not been finished yet.

I ran back to the room. He did not chase me. He only came back later, sat down at the desk, opened his notebook and wrote. His hand was very calm.

The next morning, I decided to report him to the building manager. But when I said his name, the manager frowned. He said my room had only seven people registered from the beginning of the semester. The other person had asked to move out before I moved in, an Economics student, average grades, several months behind on electricity and water bills, then disappeared. On the file there was a note: “Unable to contact.”

I laughed, because I thought he was mistaken. But when I returned to the room, the bed behind me was completely empty. No blanket, no pillow, no dark clothes. Only the thick notebook lay on the desk.

I opened it.

All the previous pages were tables tracking me. Sleep time. Time spent playing games. Number of times drawing at night. Number of times looking toward the bathroom. Level of contempt toward roommate. Level of pride in major. Level of replaceability.

The final page had only one sentence:

“The subject has met the requirements to observe himself.”

I closed the notebook.

My hand trembled.

But for some reason, the first thing I thought of was not running away. I thought about this month’s electricity bill. I thought about the guy in the next room still owing me thirty thousand for water. I thought about what his reaction would be if I reminded him right now.

I opened the notebook again.

Drew a new table.

Name.

Amount of money.

Time endured.

Level of discomfort.

When I finished writing the first line, I realized I was smiling.

That night, around nearly three in the morning, I got up from bed, left the room, then stopped in front of the bathroom door beside it. I placed my hand on the doorknob, lowered my head and listened for a while.

Outside, there was the sound of someone breathing very lightly.

I locked the door.

Click.
