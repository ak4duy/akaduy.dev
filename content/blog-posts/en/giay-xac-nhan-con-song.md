# Chapter 1: Room Number Seven

That morning, the heat was so intense that the tiles in front of city hall seemed to be silently smoking. No one could see it, but everyone felt it in the soles of their feet, in their collars, in the eyes they had to narrow against the light.

Trung arrived at a quarter to eight. He needed a document confirming that he was still alive.

The guard sat behind a wooden desk, fanning himself with an old newspaper. He did not ask who Trung was. He only pointed toward the hallway.

"Room number seven."

Trung followed the long corridor, where the pale yellow walls had swollen from dampness. Every door had a number on it. Number three. Number four. Number five. Then number eight.

There was no room number seven.

He went back to the guard.

"I can't find room number seven", Trung said.

The guard stopped fanning himself and looked at him as if he had just said something indecent.

"Of course there is."

"But I just walked past it."

"Then you didn't walk slowly enough."

Trung did not understand that sentence. But because it was hot, and because he truly needed the document, he went back down the hallway. This time, he walked more slowly. He looked carefully at each door, each crack in the wall, each dead fly lying belly-up beneath the windowsill.

Number three. Number four. Number five. Number eight.

He stood in front of number eight for a long time. Then he knocked.

A woman's voice called out.

"Come in."

Inside the room was a woman in a gray blouse, her hair tied neatly in a bun, stamping a stack of papers. The room was narrow, but the window opened onto a patch of sky that was far too bright. That light made everything in the room seem to lose its weight.

"I need a certificate confirming that I am alive", Trung said.

The woman did not look up.

"Room number seven."

"I looked. There is no such room."

She stamped three more times. Thump. Thump. Thump.

"Then you are not yet qualified to be certified as alive."

Trung looked at her hand. Her fingers were dry, quick, calm. He thought of his mother's hands when she had still been alive. She too had once had fingers like that, dry and calm, when she peeled apples for him. She died on an August afternoon. It had also been hot then, but no one had asked her to prove that she had once lived.

"What should I do?" he asked.

"Stand in line."

"Where?"

"Where everyone stands in line."

# Chapter 2: Where Everyone Stands in Line

Trung stepped outside. In the yard of city hall, a line of people had formed at some point. It stretched from the main entrance all the way to the leafless plane tree. No one was talking. A man held a chicken in a mesh bag. An old woman carried a photograph of a child. A young man wore a black suit despite the heat, sweat running from his temple down to his neck, but he did not wipe it away.

Trung stood at the end of the line.

The sun rose higher. The line moved very slowly. At times he thought it was not moving at all, that only the ground was retreating beneath everyone's feet. A child cried. The man holding the chicken told it to be quiet. The chicken did not make a sound.

At noon, a clerk came out and said:

"Room number seven is closed for lunch."

No one protested.

Trung did not protest either. He found it strange that he was not angry. Inside him there was only a bright, glaring emptiness, like the patch of sky visible through the window of room number eight. He thought perhaps anger also needed a reason, and here every reason dissolved in the heat.

The young man in the suit turned to him.

"What paper are you applying for?"

"A certificate confirming that I am alive."

"That's a hard one."

"And you?"

"I'm applying for a certificate confirming that my father is dead."

"Is he dead?"

"He died three years ago."

"Then why do you still need confirmation?"

The young man looked up at the sun.

"Without the paper, he still has to pay taxes."

Trung nodded. It seemed absurd, but no more absurd than his needing to prove he was alive to people who could see him standing under the sun.

In the afternoon, the line began to move faster. When Trung reached the door, the guard appeared, still holding the old newspaper.

"Closing time", he said.

A sigh passed through the line of people like wind through dry grass. No one left immediately. They stood there, each holding on to their own reason, as if holding a small fragile object.

Trung asked:

"Can I come back tomorrow?"

The guard shrugged.

"If you are still alive."

# Chapter 3: Shade

That night, Trung did not sleep. He lay in bed, listening to the rare passing cars outside and the clock in the next house striking each beat. He looked at his hand resting on his chest. It rose and fell with his breathing. That simple proof seemed sufficient to him. But he knew that by morning, it would not be enough.

He thought about not going back. Without the paper, he could still eat bread, drink coffee, watch birds perched on electric wires. He could still have a toothache, feel sleepy, hate the heat. He could still live.

But then he also thought that without the paper, one day people might say he had never existed. And the strange thing was, that thought did not frighten him. It only made him tired.

The next morning, Trung returned to city hall.

It was still hot. The guard was still sitting there. The line still stretched to the plane tree. He stood at the end of it, just as he had yesterday. The young man in the suit was there too, still holding the file of his dead father. The man with the chicken was also there, but today the mesh bag was empty.

"Where's the chicken?" Trung asked.

"Dead", the man replied.

"Are you applying for a paper for it?"

"No. I ate it."

Trung burst out laughing.

His laughter rang out suddenly in the yard. A few people turned to look. The guard looked too. Trung did not know why he was laughing. Perhaps because the chicken had escaped all paperwork in a way simpler than any of them. Perhaps because the sun was too bright. Perhaps because he had spent two days standing there to prove something that every breath he took had already proved, except breath did not come with a stamp.

He laughed until tears ran down his face.

Then the line moved forward.

When it was his turn, the woman in the gray blouse in room number eight handed him a sheet of paper.

"Fill this out."

Trung looked at the form.

Full name. Date of birth. Place of birth. Reason for applying for confirmation of being alive.

On the final line, he wrote: "Because I am not dead yet."

The woman read it without showing any expression. She stamped it. Thump.

"Good", she said. "Take it to room number seven."

Trung held the paper. He looked at her. She looked back at him with tired, transparent eyes. For the first time, he saw that she too was hot. She too was alive. She too might need someone to confirm it.

He stepped out into the hallway.

Number three. Number four. Number five. Number eight.

He stood still for a moment. Then he folded the paper, put it into his shirt pocket, and walked out into the yard.

The sun struck his face. The line behind him was still waiting. The guard asked:

"Where are you going?"

Trung said:

"Outside."

"You're not going to room number seven?"

Trung looked at the blinding white yard. There, the plane tree cast a patch of shade on the ground so small it was almost laughable. But it was still shade.

"I'll find it later", he said.

Then he walked toward that shadow. As he went, he placed a hand on his chest. His heart was still beating, steady, meaningless, and stubborn.

And in that moment, Trung felt that this was enough.
