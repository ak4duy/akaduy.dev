# Chapter 1: Summer

Finally, summer was almost here, the season that those ungrateful kids loved so much.

I never understood that.

To me, summer had nothing worth looking forward to besides sunlight, sweat, the smell of garbage fermenting on street corners, and faces flushed red from the heat. Adults complained about electricity bills. Students wondered whether they should keep studying or not. Every drink shop was crowded. Every road was dusty. Every bus smelled like people trapped too long inside a metal box being steamed under the sun.

And yet kids still loved it.

They ran around outside, laughing and chasing each other under sunlight that could melt asphalt. They screamed as if summer was a reward, as if three months of maddening heat was some rare kind of freedom that adults were no longer pure enough to feel.

Maybe I used to be like that too.

Maybe I used to believe summer was a blank space where everything could start over. But if I did, then that part of me had died a long time ago.

Summer exposed everything. The smell of sewers became clearer. The sound of people arguing inside rented rooms became clearer. The stains on collars became clearer. The exhausted look on people's faces when they squeezed onto buses also became clearer. Even smiles became clearer, and that was what I hated most.

Summer had too many smiles.

The smiles of students who had just finished their exams, their faces looking as if they had just escaped a small execution. The smiles of kids who got to stay home, sleep until noon, and believe that life was still on their side. The smiles of people who had not yet been taught by time that not everything beginning with sunlight ended well.

I once had a smile like that.

Or at least, there was a photo to prove it.

The photo was taken when I was eighteen, in the high school courtyard, on a morning so hot that the white shirts of everyone in class stuck to their backs. I stood near the middle of the frame, stupid hair, oily face full of acne, crooked collar, but my mouth was smiling brightly. A hateful kind of smile. Wide, bright, and full of confidence, as if the guy in the photo still did not know how filthy the future would be.

On the back of the photo, two numbers were written in blue ballpoint pen.

18 20.

No slash.

No message.

No signature.

Only two numbers standing apart by a small blank space, far enough to look like a code, close enough to make someone think they belonged together.

At first, I thought it was a date, but eighteen twenty was meaningless. Then I thought of age. Eighteen and twenty. Maybe someone wanted to send a message to the future. Maybe it was a joke from the final-year class. It could also have been me who wrote it and forgot, because humans always overestimate their ability to remember the stupid things they have done.

I examined the photo under the desk lamp, flipping it back and forth, looking for another ink stain, a faded line of writing, a folded corner. There was nothing. Only my eighteen-year-old face on the front and the two numbers on the back. The more I looked, the more uncomfortable I felt. I did not feel ashamed then, and there was nothing to be embarrassed about yet. I only hated the way the guy in the photo smiled. He smiled as if everything could still go on.

I had planned to throw the photo away.

The trash bin was right under the desk, and all I had to do was let go. But when the edge of the photo touched the mouth of the bin, I pulled it back. I told myself I was only keeping it to check later, the way people keep a piece of evidence that does not matter much but still do not want to destroy yet. In the end, I slipped it into an old book, the kind of book I bought because I thought I would read it and become more special, but in reality only used to prop up my laptop so it would not overheat.

That night, I dreamed of the school courtyard.

It did not look like the courtyard in my memory. The schoolyard back then always had the sound of cicadas, slippers scraping, desks and chairs being dragged out of classrooms. The courtyard in the dream was absolutely silent. Sunlight covered everything in a thick white, not yellow, not warm, just white like freshly painted lime. In the middle of the yard, someone stood with his back to me, wearing a school uniform shirt, thin shoulders, hair sticky with sweat.

I knew it was me at eighteen.

I knew even before he turned around.

But when he turned his head, that face had no eyes, no nose, no clear skin or flesh. All that remained was a mouth smiling, stretched so wide that the rest of the face became unnecessary.

I woke up with my throat dry and burning.

The room was sweltering even though the fan was still spinning. It felt as if someone had sat beside my bed all night, leaning down and breathing hot air into my face. I drank water, brushed my teeth, tried to wash away the unpleasant aftertaste in my mouth. While rinsing, I looked into the mirror and saw that my lips were slightly parted. My teeth were whiter than usual. That whiteness was not beautiful. It was fake, cold, like the bathroom light made everything look as if it had been bleached carelessly.

I pulled my lips down to check, then cursed myself for being insane.

An old photo.

A stupid dream.

That was all.

But when I returned to the desk, the old book was already open. The photo lay right on the tabletop, facing upward, neat as if someone had placed it there to wait for me.

I clearly remembered slipping it between the pages of the book.

There are memories humans cannot mistake. They are not that important. But when doing them, we tell ourselves: remember this, if something happens later, do not pretend you do not remember.

I picked up the photo.

The back still had the two numbers.

18 20.

The blue ink had not changed, but the distance between them looked closer. I hated myself for thinking that. Two numbers on paper could not possibly move. I took a pencil and drew a very thin line beneath each number to mark its position, then put the photo into the drawer and locked it.

That entire day, the sun felt like someone had deliberately turned it too high.

The school was becoming emptier as summer break approached. The students who remained talked and laughed more than usual, maybe because they were about to go home, or maybe because when humans stand before an empty stretch of time, they often mistake it for freedom. I walked across the schoolyard, listening to cicadas on the trees, watching groups of students taking photos, flashing peace signs, wrapping arms around each other, smiling as if their teeth were something that needed to be announced before vacation began.

I suddenly felt tired.

That tiredness did not come from the sunlight.

It came from other people's lightness.

That afternoon, the first thing I did when I got home was open the drawer.

The photo was still there.

But the pencil marks had shifted.

The line under the number 18 still stayed where I had marked it, but the number above it looked as if it had moved. Very little. So little that if I had not looked carefully, I would have missed it. I took a ruler and measured it. The distance between 18 and 20 was one point eight centimeters.

I wrote it down.

1.8 cm.

Doing that made me feel less afraid. Things with measurements still temporarily belonged to the human world. Once there was a unit, a number, a note, it was no longer an omen. It was only data.

The next night, I measured again.

1.7 cm.

On the morning of the third day, it was 1.5.

I did not tell anyone. There are things that, if spoken aloud, only lead you into one of two bad situations. If people do not believe you, you become a lunatic. If people do believe you, then it means the thing is real, and real is much more terrifying.

I kept measuring.

1.4; 1.3; 1.1.

The two numbers crawled toward each other slowly, almost politely. Not rushed. Not showy. Only a little each day, as if they knew I would check and wanted to give me enough time to understand, but not enough time to stop it.

At the same time, the smile in the photo began to change.

At first it was only a feeling. The corners of the mouth were a little higher. The teeth were a little more exposed. Then I found an old scan on my phone, placed it beside the actual photo, and every explanation collapsed very quickly. The mouth of the guy in the photo had grown wider. It was no longer the look of someone smiling more happily. It looked like the skin of his face was being pulled. Patiently. Evenly. As if someone standing outside the frame was hooking two fingers into the corners of his mouth and slowly pulling them apart.

I threw the photo onto the table.

It landed face down.

The back showed.

18 20.

The space between the two numbers was now only like a breath.

# Chapter 2: Minh

That was when I remembered Minh.

Minh was in my class in twelfth grade. He was the kind of person who always smiled in photos, not a polite smile, but a real one, smiling so much that sometimes it made other people uncomfortable. On the last day of school, he was the one who suggested a very strange game. Each person would write a message to their twenty-year-old self, put it into a paper box, then agree to open it later.

Back then the whole class went along with it because everyone thought youth was something that could be put into a box, taped shut, stored away, then opened a few years later still smelling of sunlight.

I did not remember where that box went.

I also did not remember what I wrote because it was meaningless.

But I remembered Minh dying that summer, right before starting university.

He was stabbed.

Adults said it was because of a conflict.

The word “conflict” sounded very convenient. It was short enough to fit into a local news article. Vague enough for neighbors to whisper about for a few days. And meaningless enough that no one really had to keep asking what conflict, with whom, why a boy who had just graduated, who always grinned brightly, could die from a knife wound under the ribs.

It happened after the final class gathering.

That night, everyone met at a drink shop near the school. Everyone talked more than usual, maybe because they knew that after tomorrow, no one would be forced to sit with anyone anymore. Minh brought the paper box containing the messages for our twenty-year-old selves. He kept hugging that box tightly and would not let anyone touch it.

Someone joked and asked if there was gold inside.

Minh laughed. He said:

“There is something more expensive than gold.”

No one paid attention, or maybe someone did, but when people are eighteen, strange sentences are often mistaken for showing off.

That night, Minh went home last.

The next morning, people found him in the alley behind the school, near the old wall almost completely covered with vines. He was lying on his side, one hand placed in front of his stomach, the other still gripping the handle of the paper box. Blood had soaked through his uniform shirt and turned black under the morning sun.

The box was empty. All the notes inside had disappeared.

The police asked around. His friends said they did not know. His family said Minh had never made enemies with anyone. The neighbors said they heard an argument that night, but could not recognize the voices. They only heard one very small sentence, repeated again and again.

“It is all because of you.”

No one knew whether Minh said that.

Or the other person did.

The case passed very quickly. A small case in a summer that was too hot. People said it was probably a personal conflict. Probably a fight. Probably kids these days were complicated. Probably Minh knew someone outside the class that no one knew about.

So many “probablys.”

No one was arrested. No killer. No explanation.

Meaningless.

There was only Minh’s funeral, held on a day so hot that the flowers on the altar wilted very quickly. I remembered the smell of incense, the smell of sweat, the electric fan running at full power without making anyone cooler. What I remembered most was Minh’s mother standing in front of his portrait for a long time, then saying a sentence that made all of us fall silent.

“Why is he still smiling like that?”

I looked up at the portrait. Minh in the photo was smiling brightly, so brightly that it felt strange.

Maybe because he was happy knowing he was about to die.

As if even while being stabbed, even while lying alone in the alley behind the school, Minh still saw someone bending down close to him. Someone very familiar. Someone he was not surprised to see at all.

I opened the old class group chat again.

The group had been dead for a long time, sometimes revived by reunion messages, job recruitment, insurance sales, or automatic and soulless birthday wishes. I scrolled up for a very long time, through thousands of stupid messages, old stickers, blurry photos, jokes that once made the whole class laugh but now only gave me a sense of embarrassment.

Eventually, I found the photo taken on the last day.

Minh stood beside me.

His arm around my shoulder.

I in the photo smiled brightly.

Minh smiled even brighter.

Behind us, on the classroom board, someone had written in chalk: 18/20.

I zoomed in.

Beneath that line was another smaller sentence, almost covered by someone’s head.

“Smile, there won’t be enough later.”

I looked at that sentence until my eyes stung.

Then I noticed something else.

In that photo, Minh was not looking at the camera.

He was looking at me.

Not a passing glance. Not by accident. His head was slightly tilted, his eyes shifted away from the lens, and the smile on his face did not seem meant for the person taking the photo.

It was meant for me.

I zoomed in further.

At the edge of the photo, right behind Minh, there was a person whose face had been cut off halfway. Only part of a chin, one shoulder of a white shirt, and a hand placed on the paper box Minh was holding against his chest could be seen.

That hand had a thin scar running across the joint of the thumb.

I looked down at my own hand.

The thumb joint on my right hand had the same scar.

I did not remember when I got it.

Just as I did not remember where I went that night after leaving the drink shop.

# Chapter 3: Twenty

My phone vibrated.

An unknown number sent a message. “Still remember me?”

I froze. My wrist went cold, even though the room was so hot that the back of my shirt stuck to the chair.

The second message came almost immediately. “Do not be so tense.”

Then the third. “Smile a little.”

I hurriedly turned off the screen.

The black glass reflected my face, pale, lips pressed tight. Behind that reflection, on the table, the old photo lay face up. I knew I should not turn around to look. There are things that, as long as you do not confirm them, can still remain imagination.

But then from behind me, very softly, came the sound of photo paper rubbing against the table. Like something turning itself over.

I looked into the reflection on the screen.

The eighteen-year-old me in the photo was smiling.

That thing was no longer quite a smile.

It looked more like a tear.

I wanted to stand up, wanted to throw the photo into fire, wanted to rip it into pieces so small that there would be nothing left to smile. But my legs would not move. My whole body felt nailed to the chair by the heat. The ceiling fan spun overhead, slowly, its blades cutting the air into tired circles.

The phone lit up again.

There was no sender number.

Only one line of text appeared in the middle of the screen.

“18 20.”

I turned around sharply.

The photo was still lying on the table.

The two numbers on the back of the photo had touched.

No more blank space.

No more gap.

18 stuck to 20 like two jaws biting tight.

I heard footsteps outside the hallway.

Very slow.

One step at a time.

Stopping in front of my room door.

No one knocked.

Only the sound of someone standing still outside, breathing very lightly, as if trying to hold back laughter.

I held my breath.

Under the door gap, a piece of paper was pushed in.

It slid across the tile floor and stopped right in front of my feet.

I did not want to pick it up.

But my hand automatically reached down.

It was a yellowed piece of paper, folded into fourths. The edges were brittle and dry, as if it had been lying in an old box for a very long time. On the outside was my handwriting.

To myself at twenty.

I opened it.

Inside there was only one sentence.

“If Minh refuses to return the box, do not let him say it.”

I read it again and again, until the words began to blur.

Not because I was crying.

But because I remembered.

A very small fragment.

A sweltering alley behind the school.

Minh holding the paper box against his chest.

I stood opposite him.

My hand was shaking.

Minh was no longer smiling.

He looked at me with an expression I had never seen on him before. Not angry. Not afraid. Only sad, as if he had known what I would do before I did it.

“I just want to open it", he said.

I heard my own voice answer, very strange, very low.

“No.”

“What is inside?”

I did not answer.

Minh looked down at the box.

Then he said:

“What did you write?”

“It is all because of you.”

# Chapter 4: Not Enough █████

The memory snapped there.

As if someone had cut it with a knife.

I raised my head.

The room door was still closed.

But the doorknob was turning.

Slowly.

Without a sound.

I stepped back, my back hitting the desk. The photo fell to the floor, flipping over to show its back. The two numbers 18 20 blackened and blurred, like ink freshly written. From the blank space that no longer existed between them, a very thin streak of red began to seep out.

Blood.

It crawled across the surface of the photo paper, slowly, forming a horizontal line.

Like a smiling mouth.

The door opened.

No one stood outside.

Only a pitch-dark hallway, even though there should still have been light outside. Heat poured from there into the room, carrying the smell of incense, wilted flowers, the damp wall behind the school, and blood dried on a white shirt.

Then I heard Minh’s voice.

Not from the hallway.

From behind me.

“Do you remember now?”

I did not turn around.

I did not dare.

A hand rested on my shoulder.

Heavy.

Cold.

The thumb of that hand gently rubbed the scar on my hand, as if checking an old mark.

“I was not the one who kept the box", Minh said. “It was you.”

I wanted to say no.

Wanted to say I did not remember.

Wanted to say that if it really was me, how could I have forgotten.

But my throat tightened. My mouth began to lift.

Little by little.

The two corners of my lips were stretched to the sides, slowly and patiently, as if someone were using two fingers to hook into the skin of my face and pull it out. I bit my lip until it bled. The pain made me more awake, but the smile did not stop.

It did not need my consent.

On the phone screen lying on the floor, my reflected face twisted under the pale blue light. My mouth was wider. More teeth showed. My eyes bulged, wet with tears, but the mouth continued to smile.

I looked down at the photo.

In it, Minh was no longer standing beside me.

The place where he once stood was now only a blurred patch of light, as if someone had been erased from the frame by the sun. His arm was no longer around my shoulder. But on the shoulder of the eighteen-year-old me, there was still a dark mark, like the imprint of a hand.

Then the guy in the photo slowly turned his head.

Not toward the camera.

But outward.

Toward me.

His mouth opened wide, pitch black inside, and from that darkness came Minh’s voice, so close it felt as if he were leaning beside my ear.

“Now it is your turn to return it.”

I collapsed to my knees.

The floor beneath my knees suddenly burned hot. It was no longer the tile floor of my rented room. It was rough, dusty, smelling of dry moss and sewage. I was kneeling in the alley behind the school.

The old wall covered in vines was right beside me.

On the ground, the paper box lay open.

Inside there were no letters.

No photos.

Only a small knife, its blade stained with a dark brown color that had dried a very long time ago.

Minh stood in front of me.

His uniform shirt was painfully white, except for the bloodstain beneath his ribs. That bloodstain was not flowing. It only stayed there, black and old, like a second mouth on his body.

Minh was still smiling.

But his eyes were not.

“What conflict?” he asked.

His voice was very soft.

“Who stabbed me?”

I trembled and shook my head.

I did not know.

I did not remember.

I did not want to remember.

Minh bent down and picked up the knife. He did not hand it to me. He only placed the blade in his own palm, then lightly drew it across.

No blood flowed out.

Only the sound of paper being cut.

I looked down at my hand.

The scar on my thumb split open.

From under the skin, a small piece of paper emerged, wet and crumpled, as if it had been stuffed there for many years.

I pulled it out.

On the paper was my handwriting.

“Minh knew.”

Only three words.

Three words were enough to make everything in my head collapse.

I remembered Minh’s voice in the alley.

I remembered him holding the box.

I remembered myself snatching it.

I remembered the blade.

I remembered Minh looking at me, not surprised, only sad.

And the most horrible thing was that I remembered, before he fell, he still tried to smile.

Not because he was happy.

But as if he wanted me to remember that smile for the rest of my life.

I burst into tears.

But my mouth was still smiling.

Minh placed a finger over his lips, as if soothing a child who had cried too long.

“Do not be scared", he said. “Every summer, someone gets a break.”

The next morning, they found me dead in my rented room.

No signs of forced entry.

No serious injuries.

Only a thin tear at both corners of my mouth, stretching almost to my ears, as if before dying I had tried too hard to smile.

The police wrote it down as an accident.

Slipped.

Hit my head.

Died on the spot.

On the desk, they found an old photo of twelfth grade.

In the photo, I stood near the middle of the frame.

Stupid hair.

Oily face full of acne.

Crooked collar.

But my mouth was smiling brightly.

A hateful kind of smile.

Wide, bright, and full of confidence.

As if the guy in the photo still did not know summer was looking back at him.

On the back of the photo, two numbers were written in blue ballpoint pen.

18 20.

This time, beneath those two numbers, there was another small line.

Slanted, thin handwriting, exactly like Minh’s.

“Smile.”

And below that, in my handwriting.

“There won’t be enough later.”
